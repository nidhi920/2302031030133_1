Floating Fish

Draw the outline of a fish on the bottom of a glass plate or tray in dry-erase marker.

Retrace your drawing to ensure all lines are connected, then let it dry for a minute or two.

Fill a measuring cup with tap water and pour it slowly into the dish, covering the bottom without splashing near the drawing.

Observe as the water moves toward your drawing, eventually surrounding it.

If the water splashes or the effect doesn’t work, empty the dish, erase the drawing, dry the dish, and try again.

Tilt the dish slightly from side to side and jot down your observations.

Dry erase ink is designed to be slippery, containing a chemical that makes it easily release from surfaces.

Unlike permanent markers, dry erase ink is not soluble in water and is less dense, allowing it to float.

When you tilt the dish, the fish moves around on the water’s surface.

This experiment demonstrates the buoyancy and non-solubility properties of dry erase ink.

Brush, Brush!

Write labels on sticky notes: Soda 1, Soda 2, Juice 1, and Juice 2, and place them in a row on a counter.

Fill two glasses halfway with brown soda and place them behind the Soda 1 and Soda 2 sticky notes.

Fill two glasses halfway with lemon juice and place them behind the Juice 1 and Juice 2 sticky notes.

Cover one egg with toothpaste, rubbing it until completely coated, and repeat with a second egg.

Submerge the toothpaste-covered eggs in the glasses labeled Soda 1 and Juice 1, and the remaining eggs in Soda 2 and Juice 2 glasses. Leave for 12 hours.

After 12 hours, remove the eggs from the glasses, rinse, and place each by its corresponding sticky note. Note any color differences.

Compare the strength of the eggs. The toothpaste-coated egg should be stronger, demonstrating how toothpaste protects enamel.

Solar Oven

Teach kids about solar power and sustainability with a DIY backyard solar oven.

Use it for melting crayon ends into rainbow crayons instead of cooking for food safety.

Solar ovens can get very hot and are great for educational purposes.

Sunscreen Test

Paint designs on construction paper with different sunscreens.

Leave papers in the sun and compare the results.

Display your conclusions on the fridge.

Marshmallow Catapult

Explore potential and kinetic energy by launching mini marshmallows.

Change variables to see how they affect the marshmallow's path.

A fun way to learn about science and experimentation.

Pencils Through a Bag of Water

Kids might guess that if you pierce a bag of water with a sharpened pencil, the water would all leak out. In fact, if you do it right, the polymers of the bag's plastic will re-seal around the pencil, and your counters will stay dry (and your kids will be amazed). You can get them thinking about the chemical compositions that make up everyday items.

Sticky Ice

Challenge your little scientist to lift up an ice cube with just a piece of string. It's possible ... with a little salt to help. Salt melts the ice and lowers the freezing point of the ice cube, which absorbs the heat from the water around it, making the water cold enough to re-freeze around the string.

Lava lamp

Oil and water with food coloring don't mix, teaching kids about density. For fun, add an antacid tablet, and bubbles start to flow all around like a groovy lava lamp.
